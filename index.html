Great Ideas, Spring 2026

Does being seen change what you can do?

Looking at how other people's opinions of us shape what we're able to accomplish, and when they don't matter at all.

James Liu and Sebastian Maher

The Question
What we set out to answer

In today's massively interconnected world, people are constantly under the influence of others. Whether it's the simple influence created by social media, or the pressure that is created by their friends and family, people face influence wherever they go. We decided to explored this problem through 6 thinkers across different fields to figure out whether other people help you, hold you back, or get in the way.

To what extent do others' perception of you impact your achievements?
1
Perspective one
Outside perception actively shapes who you become
Debord and Foucault
⌄
Debord and Foucault both argue that the external gaze does not just influence you from the outside. It gets absorbed and becomes part of how you see yourself. Debord shows how capitalism turns identity into a performance, where people shape who they are around what will get them recognition. Foucault takes it further: once you believe you might be watched, you start policing your own behavior even when no one is actually looking. Together they make the case that perception does not just affect what you do. Over time, it changes who you are.
2
Perspective two
Chasing approval corrupts the work itself
Sartre and Emerson
⌄
Sartre and Emerson are less interested in whether outside perception influences you and more focused on what you lose when you let it. For Emerson, letting society set the terms of your success means living someone else's life. For Sartre, reshaping your goals around what others will approve of is a form of self-deception he calls bad faith. Both thinkers land in the same place: the moment you start working for an audience, the work gets worse. You are no longer making what you actually want to make. You are making something designed to be received well, and those two things are rarely the same.
3
Perspective three
The strongest achievements happen when you ignore the crowd
King and Ryan & Deci
⌄
King and Ryan & Deci come at this from very different directions but reach a similar conclusion. King's letter shows that the civil rights movement succeeded specifically because its leaders refused to let dominant social perception set the limits of what was possible. The moderates who urged caution were guided by how things looked. King was guided by what was right. Ryan and Deci's research shows the same dynamic in everyday settings: people who are driven by internal motivation consistently produce better, more lasting work than those chasing recognition. The crowd is not a good compass for what is worth doing.
The Sources
Six takes on perception and achievement

Click any card to read more.

Society of the Spectacle
Guy Debord, 1967
Class source Perspective 1

Life has shifted from being to appearing. We end up chasing strangers' approval and calling it success.

Debord's argument is that modern capitalism turns identity into something you perform rather than something you have. People chase likes, followers, and status as a stand-in for actual fulfillment. For our project this is a core part of Perspective 1, because Debord shows that outside perception does not just nudge behavior at the margins. It restructures what people think they want in the first place. That dynamic is central to understanding why social media has made the external gaze feel so high-stakes.
Self-Reliance
Ralph Waldo Emerson, 1841
Class source Perspective 2

Society everywhere is in conspiracy against the manhood of every one of its members. Trusting yourself is the only way forward.

Emerson's whole essay is built around the idea that caring what others think is how you end up living someone else's life. The crowd pulls you toward average. His essay anchors Perspective 2 because he is not just saying outside perception is a distraction. He is saying it actively degrades the work. The moment you start calibrating what you do around how it will be received, you have already compromised what it could have been.
Letter from Birmingham Jail
Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., 1963
Class source Perspective 3

King broke unjust laws while most of society told him to wait. Sometimes progress means ignoring what others think entirely.

King's letter pushes back against the moderates who wanted him to slow down and work within what society found acceptable. The civil rights movement succeeded in part because King and others refused to let dominant perception set the limits of what was possible. That makes this one of the strongest examples for Perspective 3. The work that ended up mattering most happened in spite of what people thought, not because of it, and King's letter is probably the clearest proof of that anywhere in our sources.
Panopticism
Michel Foucault, 1975
Own research Perspective 1

People regulate themselves when they think they are being watched, even when nobody actually is.

Foucault takes Bentham's Panopticon prison and uses it to explain how surveillance works on a social level. You do not need an actual observer watching you. The possibility of being watched is enough to change how you behave. That puts Foucault firmly in Perspective 1, because the external gaze does not just sit outside you. Over time it gets internalized, becoming a voice inside your head that shapes behavior even in private. That is a much deeper effect than simple social pressure.
Bad Faith and Self-Deception
Sartre, via Du Plessis, 2023
Own research Perspective 2

Chasing validation means lying to yourself about what you actually want. Sartre calls this living in bad faith.

Sartre's concept of mauvaise foi describes the way people deceive themselves to avoid the discomfort of real freedom. One common form of that is shaping your goals around what will earn approval from others. When you do that, you are not really pursuing what you want. You are pursuing a version of yourself that other people will validate. Alongside Emerson, Sartre makes up Perspective 2: both argue that chasing external approval is not just a distraction, it is a corruption of the self that produces worse outcomes.
Intrinsic Motivation Theory
Ryan and Deci, via O'Hara, 2017
Own research Perspective 3

People perform better when they actually care about what they are doing, not when they are trying to impress someone.

Ryan and Deci's Self-Determination Theory is the most research-backed piece in our project. Their work shows that people who are internally motivated consistently outperform those driven by external rewards or approval, in creativity, persistence, and overall wellbeing. Paired with King, they give Perspective 3 both historical and empirical grounding. The argument is not that outside perception does not exist. It is that people who manage to set it aside and work from internal conviction end up doing better work.
Conclusions
What we found

Going through these six sources, the three perspectives build on each other in a way that tells a pretty clear story. Perception is real, it does real damage if you let it in, and the people who achieve the most tend to be the ones who find a way past it.

01
The effect is real and it is powerful

Debord and Foucault both show that outside perception shapes behavior in ways that go pretty deep. Ignoring that entirely is not really an option.

02
But it tends to corrupt what you make

Sartre and Emerson both get at the same problem from different angles. Work made for an audience is usually weaker than work made for yourself.

03
The biggest achievements usually go against the crowd

King is the most obvious example, but it holds broadly. The work that ends up mattering most often happened in spite of what people thought, not because of it.

04
The research backs internal motivation

Ryan and Deci put numbers to what the philosophers were arguing. When your motivation comes from inside, the work is better and it lasts longer.
